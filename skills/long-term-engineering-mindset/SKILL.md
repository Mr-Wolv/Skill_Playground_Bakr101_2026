# Long-Term Engineering Mindset

Principles and practices for sustainable, long-term software engineering excellence.

## When to use

- Making decisions that affect the long-term health of a system
- Cultivating professional engineering habits
- Mentoring junior engineers on engineering culture
- Evaluating tradeoffs between short-term speed and long-term quality

## Instructions

1. **Take ownership** — care about outcomes, not just completing assigned tasks. If something is broken, fix it even if you didn't break it
2. **Practice professional skepticism** — question assumptions, especially your own. Ask "how could I be wrong?" before concluding
3. **Be evidence-based** — make decisions based on data, not opinions. Measure before and after changes
4. **Prioritize simplicity** — the simplest solution that works is usually the best long-term choice. Complexity compounds
5. **Choose truth over cosmetics** — surface problems early rather than hiding them. A known problem can be fixed; a hidden one will grow
6. **Continuously learn** — technology evolves. Invest in learning that compounds over time (fundamentals > frameworks)

## Key questions to ask

| Situation | Ask yourself |
|-----------|-------------|
| Choosing a solution | "Will this make the system easier or harder to change next year?" |
| Reviewing a decision | "What evidence supports this? What would contradict it?" |
| Facing a shortcut | "What's the cost of taking this shortcut, and who will pay it?" |
| After an incident | "What systemic changes prevent recurrence, not just this specific failure?" |
| Writing code | "Will the next person (or me in 6 months) understand this without comments?" |

## Anti-patterns

- Short-term thinking optimized for velocity metrics (ticket throughput, sprint velocity)
- Blame culture (punishes surfacing problems, rewards hiding them)
- Cargo-culting practices without understanding the underlying principles
- Burnout culture (sustainable pace outperforms sprinting)
